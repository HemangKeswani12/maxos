# Hyperpigmentation & Post-Inflammatory Marks

## The Hierarchy

SPF > Vitamin C > Tyrosinase Inhibitors > Exfoliation > Everything else.

Without SPF, every other product is 40–60% less effective. UV stimulates melanocytes regardless of inhibitor use.

## Active Ingredient Stack

### Morning
1. Cleanser
2. **Vitamin C 15–20%** (L-ascorbic acid) — antioxidant shield, collagen cofactor, inhibits dopaquinone oxidation
3. Moisturiser
4. **SPF 50+** — non-negotiable

### Evening
Choose one primary inhibitor and rotate:

| Ingredient | Mechanism | Concentration |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| Azelaic acid | Tyrosinase inhibition, anti-inflammatory | 15–20% |
| Alpha arbutin | Slow-release hydroquinone analogue | 2% |
| Kojic acid | Copper chelation in tyrosinase | 1–2% |
| Tranexamic acid | Plasmin inhibition, anti-melanogenic | 3–5% |
| Niacinamide | Blocks melanosome transfer | 5–10% |

**Weekly:** AHA glycolic acid 10% — accelerates epidermal turnover, brings pigment to surface faster for shedding.

## Fitzpatrick Skin Type Considerations

- **Types I–III:** Respond faster. Aggressive approach tolerated.
- **Types IV–VI:** Post-inflammatory pigmentation (PIH) is the bigger concern. Avoid aggressive acids initially. Start AHA at 5%. Prioritise azelaic acid which has strong safety profile for darker skin tones.

## Timeline

| Week | Expectation |
|------|-------------|
| 0–4 | No visible change (sub-surface effect) |
| 4–8 | Slight brightening |
| 8–12 | Measurable fading of fresh PIH |
| 12–20 | Significant clearance of established marks |
| 20+ | Deep/old marks may require ongoing maintenance |

## What Doesn't Work

- **Lemon juice:** pH too acidic, UV photosensitising, no consistent evidence
- **DIY turmeric masks:** Staining, unstable curcumin
- **Vitamin C serums below 10%:** Sub-therapeutic
